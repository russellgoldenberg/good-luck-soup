Shortly after the Japanese bombing of Pearl Harbor in 1942, over 100,000 Americans of Japanese heritage were sent to internment camps. On February 19th, 1942, President Franklin D. Roosevelt signed Executive Order 9066, beginning the relocation to a dozen camps on the west coast.

In the late-1800s and early-1900s, the West Coasts of the United States and Canada experienced a surge in immigration from Japan.
Many worked as produce farmers and soon gained prominence within the agricultural community.
But, as their achievements increased so too did anti-Japanese sentiment in both politics and the public.
In 1941, the attack on Pearl Harbor led to the immediate and forced incarceration of over 120,000 Japanese Americans and over 20,000 Japanese Canadians.
After three years, the Canadian government ordered the closings of all Japanese Canadian Internment Camps in 1945.
And in the United States, the last Japanese American Internment Camp closed in 1946.
After their release from the internment camps, Japanese Americans and Japanese Canadians rebuilt their lives in various states and cities throughout North America.

Good Luck Soup is a collection of stories, gathered from across the country and generations. These stories depict the journey of Japanese Americans. Experience life before, during, and after the camps.

Each viewing will generate a unique sequence of seven chapters. A chapter will be a self-contained story, but when viewed together contribute to a universal narrative.
